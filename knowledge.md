## 2. Trading Fundamentals

### What Is Day Trading?

- **Day trading is NOT about making money.** It is about predicting where price wants to go with high probability on a daily basis. Money is the "awesome side effect" of getting good at that prediction skill.
- If your goal going into the market each day is "make money," you will overtrade, overleverge, and lose.
- If your goal is "predict where price wants to go using my confluences and strategy," you will avoid bad trades and take only high-probability setups.

### Trading Is a Skill

TJR uses three analogies repeatedly:

1. **Basketball:** You would not expect to be good at basketball after watching videos for a month without touching a ball. Similarly, you cannot learn trading by only consuming content — you must practice.
2. **Construction worker:** Given the same toolbox, blueprints, and materials, the worker who builds 5 houses a day correctly will far surpass the one who builds 1, who will far surpass the one who builds 10 but skips steps or changes blueprints every month.
3. **Health analogy:** Just like getting healthy comes from 4 basics (sleep, exercise, hydration, eating well), profitable trading comes from mastering the basics, not chasing advanced concepts.

### Key Fundamentals Taught

- **Candlestick anatomy:** Open, Close, High, Low. Body shows open-to-close; wicks show the high and low of that time period.
- **Time frames:** Each candlestick represents a specific duration (1 min, 5 min, 15 min, 1 hour, 4 hour, daily, weekly). Smaller time frames show more detail within larger candles.
- **Chart types:** Japanese candlestick charts are standard. TJR uses blue/black candle colors (purely aesthetic preference).

## 3. Chart Reading & Price Action

### Highs and Lows

- **A high** = a two-candlestick pattern: a move up then a move down (green candle followed by a red candle). Take the highest point of those two candles.
- **A low** = a two-candlestick pattern: a move down then a move up (red candle followed by a green candle). Take the lowest point of those two candles.
- **Do not overcomplicate this.** Two consecutive green candles followed by a red candle — the high is the highest point of the last green and the red. Ignore doji/flat candles.

### Trends

- **Uptrend:** Higher highs and higher lows
- **Downtrend:** Lower highs and lower lows
- **Consolidation (sideways):** No clear direction — price bounces within a range
- **Quick identification:** Just look at the chart. Is it going up, down, or sideways?
- Trends exist on every time frame. There can be a downtrend on the 5-minute inside an uptrend on the 4-hour.

### Break of Structure (BOS)

- In an **uptrend**, if a candle **closes** below the most recent low, that is a break of structure to the downside — trend has shifted bearish.
- In a **downtrend**, if a candle **closes** above the most recent high, that is a break of structure to the upside — trend has shifted bullish.
- **Must be a candle closure**, not just a wick. The body of the candle must close past the level.
- Break of structure signals a potential change in trend direction.

## 4. Confluences (Tools in the Toolbox)

TJR presents these as individual "tools" that you lay out before assembling them into a strategy. No single confluence is traded alone.

### 4.1 Liquidity

**What it is:** Resting orders (buy orders, sell orders, stop losses, take profits) in the market.

**Where it lies:**
- **Above highs** — because retail traders put stop losses above highs (shorts) and buy breakouts above highs (longs). Both create buy-side liquidity.
- **Below lows** — because retail traders put stop losses below lows (longs) and sell breakdowns below lows (shorts). Both create sell-side liquidity.

**Why it matters:**
- Liquidity is what the market is drawn toward — it acts like a magnet.
- The market needs liquidity to move and change direction. Large institutions fill their orders at these liquidity pools.
- Price sweeps above highs or below lows to fill orders, then reverses.

**Key concept:** The market is designed to go against retail. When everyone is buying above a high, institutions use that liquidity to fill massive sell orders and reverse price.

### 4.2 Order Blocks

**What it is:** A price range where large orders were filled that caused the market to change direction.

**How to identify:**
- **Bearish order block:** The leg UP that sweeps liquidity (above highs), followed by a break of structure to the downside. The order block is ONLY the move up — not the move down.
- **Bullish order block:** The leg DOWN that sweeps liquidity (below lows), followed by a break of structure to the upside. The order block is ONLY the move down — not the move up.

**How to use (continuation confluence):**
- If price revisits the order block and you see selling pressure (bearish OB) or buying pressure (bullish OB), price is likely to continue in that direction.
- Order blocks do NOT have to be revisited for price to continue moving. But if they are, it adds confluence.

### 4.3 Fair Value Gaps (FVGs) / Imbalances

**What it is:** A three-candlestick pattern showing an imbalance of orders — a price range that lacks orders in the opposite direction.

**How to identify:**
- **Bullish FVG:** Mark from the top wick of candle 1 to the bottom wick of candle 3 (in a move up). The gap between those wicks is the FVG.
- **Bearish FVG:** Mark from the bottom wick of candle 1 to the top wick of candle 3 (in a move down).
- **If the wicks overlap, there is NO fair value gap.**
- Typically associated with big candlesticks / strong moves.

**How to use (continuation confluence):**
- If price revisits the FVG and shows buying/selling pressure out of it (matching the original move direction), assume more orders were filled and price will continue.
- Not every FVG gets filled. That is normal.

**FVG Invalidation:**
- If price closes through a FVG (e.g., a bearish FVG gets a bullish candle closing above it), that FVG is invalidated.
- An invalidated FVG in the opposite direction of your bias can be used as an entry confluence.

### 4.4 Balanced Price Range (BPR)

- When a bullish FVG and a bearish FVG overlap in the same price range, the overlapping zone is a BPR.
- This creates a "high quality imbalance price range" that price is very likely to react from.
- Used primarily on execution time frames (5-min, 1-min) for more precise entries.

### 4.5 Equilibrium

**What it is:** The 50% (halfway) point from a swing high to a swing low, or vice versa. Identifies premium vs. discount pricing.

**How to identify:**
- Use a Gann Box tool on TradingView.
- In an **uptrend**: Draw from swing low to swing high. Below the 50% line = discount (where you want to buy). Above = premium.
- In a **downtrend**: Draw from swing high to swing low. Above the 50% line = discount (where you want to sell/short). Below = premium.

**How to use (continuation confluence):**
- Banks/institutions want the best price — they buy at a discount, sell at a premium.
- If price dips into the discount zone and shows buying pressure, assume price will continue higher.
- Does not have to be hit for price to keep moving. But if it does, it is higher confluence.

### 4.6 Breaker Blocks

**What it is:** A failed retracement zone — a price range where orders were filled in the previous trend direction, but price broke through it (structure break), showing those orders are depleted.

**How to identify:**
- **Bullish breaker:** The move UP prior to a break of structure to the upside (in what was previously a downtrend). This zone now lacks sell orders.
- **Bearish breaker:** The move DOWN prior to a break of structure to the downside (in what was previously an uptrend). This zone now lacks buy orders.

**How to use (continuation confluence):**
- Similar to FVGs — if price revisits the breaker and shows buying/selling pressure out of it, price likely continues in the new trend direction.
- Often overlaps with FVGs for extra confluence.

### 4.7 SMT Divergence (Smart Money Technique)

**What it is:** One index (e.g., NQ) forecasting what another index (e.g., ES) is going to do.

**How to use:**
- **Bullish SMT:** One index makes a higher low while the other makes a lower low. The one making the lower low is lagging and will likely follow the one making the higher low upward.
- **Bearish SMT:** One index makes a lower high while the other makes a higher high. The one making the higher high is lagging and will likely follow downward.

**Rules:**
- Must be in line with your overall bias first.
- When bullish, only look at lows for SMT. When bearish, only look at highs.
- Best used on the 5-minute or 15-minute time frame (not the 1-minute).
- Some traders will not take a trade without an SMT confirmation.

## 5. Trading Strategy — TJR's Systematic Step-by-Step

### Core Philosophy

> "Systemize your strategy. You should have a step-by-step plan for every trade. If you change your entry method every day, you are trading 30 different strategies and calling it one."

### The Base Strategy (Futures — AM Session)

**Step 1 — Determine Bias (4-Hour Trend)**
- Look at the 4-hour time frame. Is it in an uptrend or downtrend?
- **Uptrend = Bullish bias** (looking for longs)
- **Downtrend = Bearish bias** (looking for shorts)
- Do NOT try to predict reversals on the 4-hour. Trade with the trend.

**Step 2 — Check the 1-Hour Trend**
- The 1-hour trend determines which lower time frame you can execute on.
- **If 1-hour is IN LINE with 4-hour:** Scale down to the **5-minute** for execution.
- **If 1-hour is OPPOSITE the 4-hour:** Scale down to the **15-minute** for execution (need higher time frame confirmation).
- The 4-hour trend always overrides. If 4H is bullish and 1H is bearish, you are STILL bullish — the hourly is just retracing.

**Step 3 — Mark High Time Frame Liquidity (Draws on Liquidity)**
- If bullish: Mark out **hourly lows and 4-hour lows** — these are where price will sweep.
- If bearish: Mark out **hourly highs and 4-hour highs**.
- **Hands in your ass** — do NOTHING until those levels are hit.

**Step 4 — Wait for High Time Frame Liquidity Sweep**
- When price sweeps one of your marked levels, you can "pull your hands out."
- This can happen during pre-market, at market open, or during the session.

**Step 5 — Scale Down, Wait for Break of Structure**
- On your execution time frame (5-min or 15-min), wait for a break of structure in your bias direction.

**Step 6 — Wait for Third Confluence**
- After the break of structure, wait for price to retrace into ONE of these:
  - Order block
  - Fair value gap
  - Breaker block
  - Equilibrium

**Step 7 — Confirm with Pressure / Lower Time Frame Break**
- Wait for buying/selling pressure out of that confluence:
  - A bullish candle closing above (for longs)
  - A bearish candle closing below (for shorts)
- OR scale down one more time frame (5-min to 1-min, or 15-min to 5-min) and look for a break of structure as your entry trigger.

**Step 8 — Execute**

### Strategy Additions (Layered On)

**Addition 1 — Pre-Market Sweeps / Hourly Confluences:**
- If a liquidity sweep already happened during pre-market or London session, do not wait for another one.
- Instead, look for **hourly confluences** (hourly FVGs, order blocks, breakers, equilibrium) as your "high time frame" trigger.
- When price enters one of those hourly confluences, scale down to your execution time frame and follow steps 5-8.

**Addition 2 — High Time Frame Confluences (Beyond Liquidity Sweeps):**
- If 4H and 1H are NOT aligned: Look for **4-hour confluences** (4H FVG, 4H order block, 4H breaker, 4H equilibrium). When those get hit, scale to 15-minute.
- If 4H and 1H ARE aligned: Look for **hourly confluences** (1H FVG, 1H order block, etc.). When those get hit, scale to 5-minute.
- Liquidity sweeps are still the best setup, but this gives you more trade opportunities.

**Addition 3 — Better Entries via Lower Time Frame Scaling:**
- Instead of waiting for a full candle confirmation on your execution TF, scale down one more:
  - On the 5-minute: scale to 1-minute for a 1-min break of structure entry
  - On the 15-minute: scale to 5-minute for a 5-min break of structure entry
- This gives tighter stop losses and better risk-to-reward.

### The Strategy Is Universal (Any Time Frame)

- **Same confluences exist on every time frame** — liquidity sweeps, breaks of structure, FVGs, order blocks, breakers, equilibrium.
- **To swing trade:** Scale UP — use weekly/daily for bias, 4H/1H for execution.
- **To scalp / trade PM session:** Scale DOWN — use 15-min for bias, 5-min/1-min for execution.
- If there are no 4H or 1H setups on a given day, scale down and look for 15-min or 5-min setups.

## 6. Risk Management

### Stop Loss Placement (Three Levels)

**Level 1 — Above/Below the Liquidity Sweep (Most Conservative)**
- Place stop loss above the high (for shorts) or below the low (for longs) of the liquidity sweep.
- Rationale: If price goes past the sweep, your entire trade idea is invalid — it was not actually a liquidity sweep.
- Best risk-to-reward if your take profit is far enough.

**Level 2 — Above/Below the High/Low Made Within the Third Confluence**
- If the risk-to-reward does not work with Level 1, place the stop above/below the swing point made inside the confluence zone (e.g., the high formed inside the FVG before the bearish candle).

**Level 3 — Above/Below the Confluence Itself**
- If you want to give the trade more breathing room, place the stop above the entire confluence zone.
- Price can push deeper into a confluence before reversing — this accommodates that.
- Worst risk-to-reward of the three, but most room for the trade to work.

### Take Profit Placement

- **Never move or extend take profits.** Set them and leave them.
- **Never move stop losses.** Set and forget.
- Take profits are based on **high time frame confluences where price could reverse:**
  - Hourly draws on liquidity (lows for shorts, highs for longs)
  - 4-hour draws on liquidity
  - 4-hour/hourly order blocks, FVGs, breakers
- **Match take profit time frame to entry time frame:**
  - Entering on the 5-minute? Take profits on hourly/4-hour levels.
  - Entering on the 1-minute (PM session)? Take profits on 15-minute/5-minute levels.
  - Do NOT set 5-minute entries with daily take profit targets — too much can happen in between.
- **Multiple take profits:** Exit partial positions at successive levels (e.g., TP1 at hourly lows, TP2 at 4-hour lows, TP3 at 4-hour order block).

### General Risk Rules

- **Risk 1% per trade** (mentioned as standard; TJR focuses more on the structural placement of stops than a fixed dollar amount).
- **Do not overleverge.** If you cannot control this, you have a life issue bigger than trading.
- **Do not overtrade.** Follow the system. If there is no setup, do not trade.
- **Trade on demo first.** You would not start in the NBA without practice games.
- If you are on a live account and you do not have a proven strategy, you will "get eaten alive."

## 8. Daily Routine / Trading Plan

### Trading Sessions & Kill Zones

**AM Session (Primary — Recommended)**
- **Pre-market:** 8:30 AM ET — Activity starts but do NOT trade during pre-market.
- **Market open:** 9:30 AM ET
- **Kill zone:** **9:50 AM - 10:10 AM ET** — This is the optimal window for entries. The 4-hour candle closes at 10:00 AM, causing increased volatility.
- During 9:30-9:50, price is typically moving toward a draw on liquidity or developing. The actual entry opportunity usually comes in the kill zone.
- **Never try to execute before 9:50 AM.**

**PM Session (Alternative)**
- **Kill zone:** **1:30 PM - 3:00 PM ET** (also cited as 1:50 PM - 2:10 PM for the sharpest moves)
- Lower volatility than AM session. Smaller candles, slower moves.
- PM session often continues the move that AM session started.
- PM session loves to sweep out the "gritty, grindy" lows/highs created during the AM-to-PM transition.
- Use lower time frames: 15-min for trend/bias, 5-min for liquidity sweeps, 1-min for execution.
- Set only ONE take profit (smaller moves = less room for multiple TPs).

### Pre-Market Routine

1. Check 4-hour trend → determine bullish or bearish bias.
2. Check 1-hour trend → determine execution time frame (5-min or 15-min).
3. Mark out high time frame draws on liquidity (hourly highs/lows, 4-hour highs/lows).
4. Check if any sweeps already happened during pre-market or London session.
5. **Hands in your ass** until a level gets hit. Do not force trades.

### TJR's Approach to Catching 1-3 Hour Moves

- TJR aims to catch **1 to 3-hour moves** on the intraday time frame.
- He does NOT care about the daily or weekly bias for intraday — the 4-hour and 1-hour are sufficient.
- He stays in the market for approximately 1-3 hours per trade.
- He typically takes **1-2 trades per day** maximum. Some days, zero trades.

## 9. Common Mistakes

1. **Having multiple strategies.** Pick one. If you are using TJR's strategy on Monday, some random TikTok strategy on Tuesday, and ICT's approach on Wednesday, you are trading 3 different strategies and will never master any.

2. **Having multiple mentors.** Each mentor teaches confluences differently. Mixing them creates confusion. Choose one person and learn their system completely.

3. **Overthinking and overcomplicating.** You do not need to analyze the weekly, daily, 4-hour, hourly, 15-minute, 5-minute, AND 1-minute. You do not need 10 confluences. Keep it simple.

4. **Looking at too many time frames.** TJR's strategy uses only 4 time frames: 4-hour, 1-hour, 5-minute (or 15-minute), and 1-minute.

5. **Overlearning and underpracticing.** Watching 100 YouTube videos without ever back-testing or taking practice trades is the biggest waste of time. Practice > consumption.

6. **Trying to get rich quick.** If you expect to make $100K in your first month, you will rage quit. Think in years.

7. **Giving up too soon.** The biggest failure is quitting. Most traders who "fail" simply stopped trying.

8. **Not systemizing your strategy.** If you enter differently every day (different time frames, different confluences, different bias method), you have no way to identify what is working and what is not.

9. **Jumping into live accounts too early.** Trade demo first. Get reps in games that do not matter before playing in the championship.

10. **Moving stop losses and take profits.** Set them and leave them. Emotion-driven adjustments destroy your edge.

11. **Trading before 9:50 AM.** Price is still developing. Wait for the kill zone.

12. **Trading during no-setup days.** Some days there is simply no trade. That is okay. Forcing a trade is worse than no trade.

13. **Entering off a single confluence.** An order block alone is not a trade. A FVG alone is not a trade. You need the full system: bias + liquidity sweep + break of structure + third confluence + confirmation.

## Quick Reference: The Strategy Checklist

```
BEFORE MARKET OPEN:
[ ] Check 4H trend → Bullish or Bearish? (This is your bias)
[ ] Check 1H trend → In line with 4H? 
    → Yes: Execute on 5-min
    → No:  Execute on 15-min
[ ] Mark high time frame liquidity levels (1H and 4H highs/lows)
[ ] Check if pre-market already caused a sweep

DURING SESSION (9:50 AM - 10:10 AM kill zone):
[ ] Wait for high time frame liquidity sweep OR high time frame confluence hit
[ ] Scale to execution time frame (5-min or 15-min)
[ ] Wait for break of structure in bias direction
[ ] Wait for third confluence (FVG, order block, breaker, equilibrium)
[ ] Confirm: bullish/bearish candle out of confluence OR lower TF break of structure
[ ] Execute trade
[ ] Stop loss: Above/below liquidity sweep, or confluence zone
[ ] Take profit: At high time frame draws on liquidity / confluences

AFTER TRADE:
[ ] Do NOT move stop loss or take profit
[ ] Journal the trade — win or loss
[ ] Identify what worked and what didn't
```
